Vector Embeddings & Similarity Scores

A RAG Masterclass: Understanding the math behind semantic search.

1. What are Vector Embeddings?

In modern Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems, traditional keyword matching (e.g., searching for exactly "error code 543") is often insufficient. We need systems that understand semantic meaning—where a search for "dog" also brings up "canine." This capability is powered entirely by vector embeddings and the math used to compare them.

An embedding is a representation of data (text, images, audio) as an array of numbers (a high-dimensional vector) that captures its fundamental semantic meaning. Embedding models map words, sentences, or entire document chunks into a dense vector space. These models are trained so that concepts with similar meanings result in vectors that are placed close to each other in this multidimensional space.

Example: If the word "king" is encoded as [0.25, 0.75] and "queen" is [0.23, 0.77], their numerical closeness reflects their strong semantic relationship.

2. The Big Three: Measuring Similarity

Once data is transformed into embeddings, inference becomes pure vector math. Here are the three primary ways to measure the distance between your query and your documents.

Cosine Similarity

Measures the angle between two vectors, completely ignoring their overall magnitude (length).

  • Best for: Text embeddings. Isolates directional alignment.
  • Range: 1 (identical) to -1 (opposite). 0 is orthogonal.
$$\cos(\theta) = \frac{\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B}}{\|\mathbf{A}\| \|\mathbf{B}\|}$$

Euclidean Distance (L2)

Measures the straight-line physical distance between two points in multi-dimensional space.

  • Best for: Spatial concepts, clustering (K-Means).
  • Range: 0 (identical) to infinity. Smaller is better.
$$d(\mathbf{A}, \mathbf{B}) = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (A_i - B_i)^2}$$

Dot Product

Measures a combination of direction and magnitude.

  • Best for: When length signifies importance.
  • Note: Identical to Cosine if vectors are normalized (length 1).
$$\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} A_i B_i$$

3. Real-World RAG Example: Sentence Similarity

How does this math actually apply to text? Let's walk through a simplified Retrieval-Augmented Generation scenario where a user asks a question, and the system must find the most relevant document chunk.

Step 1: The User Query

The user types a question. The embedding model (e.g., OpenAI's text-embedding-3-small) converts this phrase into a 1536-dimensional vector.

Query: "How do I reset my forgotten password?"
Vector Q: [0.015, -0.022, 0.081, 0.003, ... 1532 more dimensions]

Step 2: The Document Chunks

Your vector database contains thousands of pre-embedded text chunks. Let's look at two of them:

Chunk A (Relevant)

"To recover account access, navigate to user settings and click 'Send Recovery Email'."

Vector A: [0.018, -0.020, 0.075, 0.011, ...]
Chunk B (Irrelevant)

"The company reported a 15% increase in Q3 revenue due to enterprise sales."

Vector B: [-0.041, 0.088, -0.012, -0.033, ...]

Step 3: Calculating Similarity

The Vector Database uses Cosine Similarity to compare Vector Q against Vector A and Vector B by calculating the angles between them in 1536-dimensional space.

Score A: 0.89
Vectors point in roughly the same direction. The semantic concepts of "reset/forgotten/password" align perfectly with "recover/access/email", even with zero keyword overlap.
Score B: 0.12
Vectors are nearly orthogonal. The geometric distance tells the system these topics are unrelated.
Result: Chunk A is retrieved and injected into the LLM's prompt context to accurately answer the user's question!
